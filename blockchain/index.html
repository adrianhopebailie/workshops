Problems and prospects for the role of standards in distributed ledger ecologies

Blockchains are in the being used in an increasing variety of places… what does this mean for Web technologies?

Distributed ledgers rose to prominence as part of the Bitcoin cryptocurrency, which includes not only a digital currency, but a fully-distributed (e.g. non-centralized) way of transferring and settling accounts through a protocol for announcing transactions, an algorithm for validating them by multiple competing and cooperating servers (called the “proof-of-work” in Bitcoin), and an immutable timestamped public record for verifying them (called the “blockchain” in Bitcoin).

The blockchain and similar structures can record other data besides payment transactions, including identity management, contracts, deeds, patents, correspondance, and anything a user might want to have as a verifiable, timestamped assertion.

In addition to its use in digital currency, this non-monetary use of blockchains (more generally called “distributed ledgers”) has generated enormous programming efforts and hundreds of millions of dollars of investment in the past couple of years, and interest seems to be increasing, especially from larger financial institutions.

Many projects and companies are now exploring to blockchain solutions to solve a variety of issues with transactions and records on the Internet and Web.

W3C sees the potential value in blockchains of various types, and is holding a workshop, reaching out to our membership and convening experts to explore possible linkages between Web and blockchain. What does the Web ecosystem needs to fully realize the potential of blockchain technology?

This workshop is intended to determine how blockchain could extend, enhance, or increase the Web Platform functionality, and what blockchain needs from the Web Platform, to identify opportunities for interaction with ongoing standards work and to suggest the development of new work to fill gaps in the current platform. We expect to include presentations of existing work inside and outside W3C, and proposals for new work and collaboration.

The Challenge

In any potential standardization of distributed ledgers, W3C wants to make sure that we have space for systems both permissioned and permissionless, with our without associated currencies, and with or without executing logic.

We want to identify the biggest challenges, most compelling use cases, and most promising solutions for standardization, and to understand the timelines for development and standardization. We invite you to submit a paper and to attend this workshop to help shape the next steps.

Why W3C?

What does W3C have to do with blockchain and Bitcoin? Is W3C trying to force premature standardization, or to “take over” blockchain?

No, W3C recognizes that there is an active and thriving blockchain community, with its own consensus process, goals, and timelines, and we don't want to get in the way of that. By holding this blockchain workshop, we're only trying to contribute to the existing conversation, and to investigate if there are ways in which our field of expertise, the Web, can be adapted to move the technology forward. W3C operates by providing a framework for organizations and communities to reach agreement and build interoperability around emerging technologies, so we hope to be useful to existing blockchain efforts, where appropriate. Work on standards will only emerge if and when those communities feel W3C has something to contribute.

Workshop topics

Possible topics include, but are not limited to the following:

  • Smart contracts and conditional execution contexts
  • Blockchain APIs
  • Blockchain primitives, such as transaction initiation, key signing, wallet management
  • Identity systems, including privacy and confidentiality factors
  • Voting systems
  • Survey of existing blockchain software systems
  • …

Who Should Attend

  • Representatives from the Bitcoin community, and related communities such as Hyperledger and Ethereum
  • Browser developers interested in adding support for blockchain APIs, identity systems, and other functionality
  • Digital currency projects
  • Security researchers
  • Financial institutions
  • Developers of blockchain systems who want to improve interoperability

How to Participate

If you are working on blockchain or distributed ledger technologies, we want you to attend this workshop.

We want to know more about your state-of-the-art approaches.

  • Share your knowledge of the topics or other areas of interest to you.
  • Offer your experiences in using blockchain approaches.
  • Demonstrate prototypes of emerging technologies that could benefit from global Web standards.
  • Show research results or lessons learned to ensure others don't reinvent the wheel.

The Call for Papers is not yet open.

Registration is not yet open. Registration is free.

To ensure productive discussions, the workshop is limited to 60 attendees. Participation is free and open to W3C members and non-members. Each organization may provide a maximum of two attendees.

Position papers or statements of interest are required to be eligible to participate in this workshop. Organizations or individuals wishing to attend must submit a position paper explaining their perspectives on a workshop topic of their choice no later than 20 May 2014. Participants should have an active interest in the area selected, ensuring other workshop attendees will benefit from the topic and their presence.

Position papers should:

  • Explain the participant's perspective on the topic of the Workshop
  • Explain their viewpoint
  • Include concrete examples of their suggestions
  • Explain how their position relates directly to the Web or browser-facing features

The authors of particularly salient or representative submissions will be given the opportunity to present their position at the workshop, to foster discussion. Those submitters not selected to present are still encouraged to attend the workshop to contribute to the discussion.

Position papers must be in English, and HTML format is strongly preferred, though we will also accept plain text or PDF format; presentation slides are also preferred to be in HTML, but other slide formats are acceptable. All submissions should be a few paragraphs to two pages in length (approximately 500–2000 words), although they may link to longer versions or appendices.

Please note that all submitted position papers will be published on the public Web page of the workshop.

Position papers should be submitted via email to the <team-blockchain-submission@w3.org> mailing list.

Event Archive Policy: Video and Transcripts

For posterity and for those unable to attend this workshop, we may be recording the video and/or audio of the event, and will provide a live IRC transcript of the presentations and group discussion. Participants will be asked to sign a media waiver.

Schedule

The workshop will focus around several topics identified by the position papers. Each topic session starts with 1–3 five-minute lightning talks, followed by 30–45 minutes of discussion. The goal of the discussion is not be to resolve the technical issues of the topic, but to determine its relevance and priority to standardization. The workshop day will end with a session on establishing the planned deliverables of a proposed Web Blockchain Working Group charter.
